AI Newsroom

An agentic system that scouts, fact-checks, and narrates a daily AI news podcast on its own. Built solo on the Claude Agent SDK. Runs free, on a schedule, no server.

Scout → Editor → Writer → Fact-checker → Producer
Read the case study →

Not one model call in a costume. A newsroom of five specialized agents that hand work to each other and argue before they publish.

The honest part: the agents were the easy twenty percent. Every real fight was in the glue. The free voice that worked on my Mac and got blocked from the cloud. The run that reported success and silently sent nothing. That is the real story of shipping AI.

The problem worth solving

AI moves faster than anyone can keep up with, and the people who most need to stay current have the least time to read. The gap isn't access to news — it's time. So I designed around the one slot left in the day: the commute. That single constraint set the format (audio), the length (short), and the tone (a podcast, not a wall of bullets).

Where it broke

The free voice worked on my Mac and died in the cloud, because Microsoft blocks edge-tts from datacenter IPs, so I moved the audio stage to Gemini TTS. A run reported success and sent nothing, because the code was swallowing errors, so I rebuilt it to fail loudly. A WAV versus MP3 mismatch broke delivery until I converted it — which also shrank a 19 MB file to email size. Almost none of these were the agent logic. The plumbing was the other eighty.

About

Product Manager Builder

enterprise trained, startup tested

I take things from real problem to shipped product.
That's always been the through-line — and I'm just getting started.

I have my MBA from Michigan State's Broad College of Business. Before that I was in engineering — building systems, writing code, thinking in edge cases. The move into product wasn't a pivot. It was the same instinct applied to bigger problems.

I've spent the last two years at the edge of AI: shipping agentic systems, testing what vibe-coding actually teaches you about judgment, and figuring out what it means to build products when the tools themselves are still in flux.

The through-line: I'm not interested in describing problems. I'm interested in the decisions you have to make to get to a working, shipped thing — and in how you know when you're done.

Outside work: long walks, books that make me think differently, and asking "why does this product exist" about everything I use.
